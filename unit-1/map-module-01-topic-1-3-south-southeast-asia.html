Two Maps, Read Together

Map Notes

Two Worlds, One Region

Topic 1.3 requires students to think across South Asia, mainland Southeast Asia, and maritime Southeast Asia. These two maps focus on Southeast Asia’s mainland and maritime patterns while connecting those patterns back to South Asia’s Delhi Sultanate and Vijayanagara Empire.

The Khmer Empire map shows a land-based empire centered at Angkor. Its power depended on rice agriculture, river systems, water management, and Hindu-Buddhist kingship. The Majapahit Empire map shows a maritime power centered on Java that influenced island and strait routes in the Indonesian archipelago. Its power depended on spice trade, naval reach, and commercial networks.

Read these maps together: one shows land power; the other shows sea power. Both are essential for understanding how South and Southeast Asia connected to the wider world.

Map Key & Regional Context

Delhi Sultanate South AsiaThe first major Islamic state on the Indian subcontinent, established in 1206 CE.
Vijayanagara Empire South IndiaA major Hindu kingdom established in 1336 in the southern Deccan.
Khmer Empire Mainland Southeast AsiaCentered at Angkor in modern Cambodia and shaped by river systems, rice agriculture, and water management.
Majapahit Empire Maritime Southeast AsiaA Javanese maritime empire connected to island trade and routes near the Strait of Malacca.
Strait of MalaccaA narrow chokepoint between the Malay Peninsula and Sumatra through which much maritime trade passed.
Monsoon Wind RoutesSeasonal wind systems that made long-distance Indian Ocean travel more predictable.

Geographic Takeaway

This region’s diversity was shaped by geography: monsoon winds made the Indian Ocean a crossroads, and crossroads produce cultural mixing, not cultural uniformity.

Map Question

How did geography shape religious and political diversity?

AP Skill: Causation & Comparison

Using both maps, explain how the Indian Ocean, the Strait of Malacca, monsoon winds, and the division between mainland and maritime zones helped cause religious and cultural diversity in South and Southeast Asia between 1200 and 1450.

  • Why does the Strait of Malacca matter to states and merchants?
  • How did monsoon winds shape who came to Southeast Asia?
  • Why is Angkor land-based while Majapahit is maritime?
  • Why did Islam spread into much of Southeast Asia by sea?

Write 3 to 5 sentences. Reference both maps and use at least one specific geographic feature.
