Topic 7.4 — Economy in the Interwar Period  |  AP World History: Modern

First & 10 Reading

When the Money Stopped

Total war left the world's economies exhausted; then, in October 1929, the American stock market crashed and the exhaustion became collapse. Unemployment, business failures, and a global trading system in ruins forced every kind of government to answer the same question: what is the state's job when the economy fails? The era of hands-off government was over. A democracy, a fascist dictatorship, a communist state, and two popular-nationalist governments in Latin America gave four very different answers — and comparing them is exactly what this topic asks of you.

Causation Comparison Argumentation

Before You Read

Hold one similarity and many differences. The similarity is the Key Concept itself: after WWI and the Depression, every government took a more active role in economic life. The differences are ideological — track for each response: who controls the economy, who benefits, and what happens to individual rights. The Soviet case gets its own Key Concept; give its human cost special attention.

Reading Target

By the end, you should be able to explain why governments intervened after World War I and the onset of the Great Depression, explain how the Soviet Five-Year Plans controlled the economy and what they cost the population, and compare the New Deal, the fascist corporatist economy, and the state-led economies of Brazil and Mexico.

Contextualization

The Bill Comes Due

The total war of Topic 7.3 was an economic event as much as a military one. Four years of feeding whole societies into the front left Europe exhausted — governments buried in debt, currencies inflated, trade networks broken, and recovery propped up by American loans. The world economy of the 1920s was a convalescent leaning on one crutch.

In October 1929 the crutch snapped. The stock market crash in New York triggered the Great Depression: banks failed, credit vanished, and the disaster traveled outward along every artery of the connected world economy. By 1932, unemployment reached a quarter of the workforce in the United States and Germany; business failures spread by the tens of thousands; global trade collapsed to a fraction of its former volume. Coffee rotted in Brazil while bread lines lengthened in Berlin — the market had failed everywhere at once.

The crisis ended an era. Through the 19th century, the reigning answer had been laissez-faire — government keeps its hands off the economy. After 1929 that answer was politically dead. The Key Concept states the turn precisely: following World War I and the onset of the Great Depression, governments began to take a more active role in economic life. The question was no longer whether to intervene. It was how — and the answers split along ideology.

AP Thinking — Causation

Chain the causes in order: total war exhausts the world economy → the 1929 crash turns exhaustion into collapse → unemployment, business failures, and collapsing trade discredit hands-off government → states intervene. On the exam, the Depression is both an effect (of WWI's economic damage) and a cause (of intervention everywhere — and, as Topic 7.6 will show, of the next war).

Key Concept

Two Answers in the West: The New Deal and the Corporatist Economy

The first illustrative example is the New Deal — the democratic answer. Franklin D. Roosevelt's program promised relief, recovery, reform: the Social Security Act built a permanent safety net for the old and unemployed; the Works Progress Administration (WPA) put millions directly on the government payroll, building roads, bridges, schools — even murals and plays. The New Deal did not abolish capitalism or suspend the Constitution; it permanently expanded government's role inside a democracy, with courts, elections, and unions intact.

Mussolini's Italy gave the authoritarian answer: the fascist corporatist economy. The state organized corporations — sector-by-sector bodies partnering government with major industries — to direct production toward nationalist goals. Independent labor unions were curtailed and absorbed into state syndicates; strikes were banned. Fascist economics pursued self-sufficiency and national power — rearmament, prestige projects, autarky — and paid for them with individual rights. Intervention here served the state, not the citizen.

AP Thinking — Comparison

Here is your first precise comparison pair. Similarity: both the New Deal and the corporatist economy put the state deep into economic life — public payrolls, industrial policy, social programs. Difference: the New Deal worked within democratic accountability (elections, courts, unions survived); the corporatist economy dismantled exactly those checks. The difference is not degree of intervention — it is what the intervention served.

3

Key Concept

The Soviet Five-Year Plans — Control and Its Cost

The Soviet Union gets its own Key Concept, and the wording deserves close reading: the government controlled the national economy through the Five-Year Plans, often implementing repressive policies, with negative repercussions for the population. Stalin's first Five-Year Plan (1928–1932) — launched even before the Wall Street crash — aimed at rapid industrialization at any cost: steel mills, dams, tractor factories, entire cities like Magnitogorsk willed into existence by state command.

The cost fell on the countryside. Forced collectivization seized peasant land and livestock into state farms; peasant resistance — millions slaughtered their animals rather than surrender them — was answered with deportations to forced labor camps. Grain quotas stripped villages bare while the state exported food to buy machinery. In Ukraine the result was the Holodomor: a famine that killed millions in 1932–1933 while the harvests that could have saved them were sold abroad.

Hold both halves of the truth. The Plans worked, by their own measure: the USSR industrialized in a decade at a speed no market economy had matched — which is why the Soviet model looked attractive to many observers during the capitalist Depression. But the Key Concept's verdict is the one to remember: state control prioritized output over human well-being, and the population paid.

AP Thinking — Argumentation

Beware the efficiency trap. A claim like "the Five-Year Plans were the most effective Depression response" needs a definition of effective: fastest industrial growth, yes — but purchased with collectivization, forced labor, and famine. A strong argument states its criteria (growth? rights? human cost?) before ranking the responses. The CED hands you the counterweight: "repressive policies, with negative repercussions for the population."

4

Key Concept

Brazil, Mexico, and the Comparison Frame

The third illustrative example often gets skipped — do not skip it: governments with strong popular support in Brazil and Mexico. In Brazil, Getúlio Vargas answered the collapse of coffee prices with a state-led national economy: state-owned enterprises in steel and oil, and import substitution industrialization (ISI) — building at home the manufactured goods Brazil had always imported.

Mexico's intervention delivered the unfinished promises of its Revolution — the one you studied in Topic 7.1. Under Lázaro Cárdenas, the government carried out sweeping land reform, distributing tens of millions of acres to villages, finally honoring the demand Zapata had died for. Then in 1938 Cárdenas nationalized the oil industry, expelling foreign companies and founding Pemex — a state oil monopoly that made economic sovereignty a matter of national pride. Crowds filled the Zócalo to donate jewelry and chickens toward the compensation payments: intervention with strong popular support, exactly as the CED says.

Now assemble the comparison frame this topic is built for: four answers to one crisis — democratic (New Deal), fascist (corporatist economy), communist (Five-Year Plans), and popular-nationalist (Brazil and Mexico). The similarity is universal: a more active state. The differences are ideological: who controls, who benefits, what happens to rights. And keep the thread for Topic 7.6 — the Depression's despair is fuel for the extremism that causes the next war.

AP Thinking — Comparison

The CED's illustrative examples ARE the comparison set — use them by name. One sentence each on the New Deal, the fascist corporatist economy, and Brazil/Mexico, plus the Soviet Key Concept, gives you four ideologically distinct data points for the same phenomenon: government taking a more active role in economic life. That is a ready-made continuity-and-comparison paragraph for the Unit 7 essay.

BeReady: 10-Second Takeaway

After World War I exhausted the world's economies, the 1929 crash brought the Great Depression — unemployment, business failures, collapsing global trade — and ended the era of hands-off government: governments began to take a more active role in economic life. The answers split by ideology. The New Deal (Social Security, WPA) expanded the state inside American democracy. Mussolini's fascist corporatist economy fused state and industry while curtailing unions and rights. Stalin's Five-Year Plans put the whole Soviet economy under state command — rapid industrialization bought with forced collectivization, forced labor, and the Holodomor famine: repressive policies with negative repercussions for the population. And in Latin America, Vargas's Brazil (state enterprises, ISI) and Cárdenas's Mexico (land reform, the 1938 oil nationalization that created Pemex) built state-led economies with strong popular support. One crisis, four answers — same intervention, different ideologies, very different human costs.
