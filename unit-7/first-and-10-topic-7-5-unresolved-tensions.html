Topic 7.5 — Unresolved Tensions After World War I  |  AP World History: Modern

First & 10 Reading

The Empire That Would Not End

The empires you watched collapse after 1900 left their colonies on the table, and the victors of World War I did not hand them back — they redistributed them. Between the wars, Western and Japanese imperial states predominantly maintained the control they had, gained more through mandates and conquest, and faced a rising tide of anti-imperial resistance. The Learning Objective asks the question directly: what changed in territorial holdings — and what stayed exactly the same?

Continuity & Change Comparison Argumentation

Before You Read

Hold two ideas at once: continuity (imperial control was predominantly maintained) and change (territory was gained through mandates and conquest, and resistance was rising). For every development, ask: is this control MAINTAINED, territory GAINED, or empire CHALLENGED? Sort everything into those three buckets and the topic snaps into focus.

Reading Target

By the end, you should be able to explain how Western and Japanese imperial states maintained and expanded control between the wars, how the League of Nations mandate system transferred former German and Ottoman territories to Britain and France, and how the Indian National Congress and West African resistance challenged imperial rule.

Contextualization

A Peace That Settled Nothing

In Topic 7.1 you watched empires crack — the Ottoman, German, Austro-Hungarian, and Russian empires all collapsed in the war's aftermath, leaving vast stretches of territory on the table. In Topics 7.2 and 7.3 you saw the total war that brought them down. When the guns finally stopped in 1918, the map of the world was waiting to be redrawn — and the victors held the pen.

Here is the move that catches students off guard: those colonies did not become free. The empires of the losers were absorbed by the winners. Between the two world wars, Western and Japanese imperial states predominantly maintained control over the colonial holdings they already had — and several of them gained more. The Learning Objective frames the whole topic as a question of continuities and changes in territorial holdings: what changed, and what stayed the same?

The core claim is the one to carry through every section: empires did not end in 1918 — they reorganized. The war redrew borders and changed which flag flew where, but the underlying fact of imperial control held. What changed sat on top of a deep continuity; what stayed the same generated tensions that the peace left unresolved.

AP Thinking — Continuity & Change

Train yourself to sort every development into one of three buckets. MAINTAINED (continuity): Britain and France keep their pre-war empires. GAINED (change): mandates and Japanese conquest add new territory. CHALLENGED (change): the Indian National Congress and West African resistance push back. A continuity-and-change essay is really just naming what persisted, naming what shifted, and explaining how they relate.

2

Key Concept

The Mandate System: Empire by Another Name

The first illustrative example of territorial gain is the League of Nations mandate system. Former German colonies were transferred to Great Britain and France — German East Africa (Tanzania) to Britain, German South West Africa (Namibia) to South African control. On paper this was not conquest; it was international trusteeship, blessed by the new League of Nations.

Listen to the rhetoric: the mandates existed, the Covenant said, to prepare territories for self-governance — to hold them in trust until their peoples were "able to stand by themselves." The reality was continued colonial exploitation. The same resources were extracted, the same populations governed without consent, the same flag of empire flew — only the legal label had changed. The mandate system is the Key Concept's "additional territories through treaty settlement" wearing the costume of humanitarian progress.

The pattern repeated in the former Ottoman lands. The wartime Sykes-Picot Agreement (1916) had already secretly divided the Middle East into British and French spheres; postwar mandates made it official, handing Syria and Lebanon to France and Iraq to Britain. Borders were drawn without regard to ethnic and sectarian boundaries — straight lines serving imperial convenience, sowing future conflict that would outlast the century.

AP Thinking — Argumentation

Watch the gap between word and deed. The mandate system claimed to be a step TOWARD independence; in practice it was a tool of continued control. A strong argument names that contradiction explicitly: the rhetoric of "tutelage" and "sacred trust" legitimized empire precisely when colonized peoples were demanding the opposite. Quote the gap, don't just describe the policy.

3

Key Concept

Japan's Expansion — Gains by Conquest

Not every gain came dressed as a mandate. Japan was an imperial state that expanded the old-fashioned way — by conquest. In 1931 its army seized Manchuria, and in 1932 it installed Manchukuo, a puppet state with a borrowed emperor and a flag of its own, but with every real decision made in Tokyo.

Japan wrapped its expansion in anti-imperial language. The Greater East Asia Co-Prosperity Sphere was advertised as Asian unity against Western imperialism — Asia for the Asians, free of European masters. In practice it served Japanese interests: a new empire built on the rhetoric of ending empires. The conquest drove rising tensions with China and the Western powers that the fragile interwar order could not contain.

This is the Key Concept's other route to "additional territories": where mandates were gains by treaty settlement, Manchukuo was a gain by conquest. And it points forward — Japanese expansion in East Asia is one of the lit fuses that foreshadows the next war in Topic 7.6.

AP Thinking — Comparison

Compare the two paths to gain. Similarity: both the mandate system and Manchukuo expanded imperial control after WWI. Difference: mandates gained territory by treaty and called it trusteeship; Japan gained territory by conquest and called it liberation. Both used high-minded language to disguise the same fact — empire growing, not shrinking. Naming that shared disguise is a sophisticated comparison.

4

Key Concept

The Resistance: India, West Africa, and the Change Beneath

Against the continuity of maintained empire ran the change the Key Concept names last: anti-imperial resistance. In India, the Indian National Congress — founded back in 1885 — moved from polite petitions toward mass protest, learning to mobilize an entire subcontinent. Postwar discontent exploded after the Jallianwala Bagh massacre (1919), when British troops fired on an unarmed crowd at Amritsar, killing hundreds. The massacre galvanized the broader independence movement, turning moderate grievance into a mass demand for self-rule.

In French West Africa, resistance took the shape of strikes, protests, and congresses challenging French colonial rule. These were organized political acts — workers and educated elites building institutions to press their claims — and beneath them grew a nationalist sentiment and a demand for self-determination that echoed from Amritsar to Dakar.

Now assemble the synthesis the Learning Objective wants: imperial control was predominantly maintained (continuity) even as mandates and conquest redrew the map (change) and resistance built pressure (change). The tensions this left unresolved — arbitrary borders, conquered peoples, colonized populations demanding freedom — are the fuse for Topic 7.6, and a thread to carry into the 7.9 causation capstone.

AP Thinking — Continuity & Change

The resistance IS the change — use it by name. The Indian National Congress and West African strikes and congresses are the CED's required evidence that maintained control was being challenged. Pair them against the continuity of empire and you have a complete continuity-and-change paragraph: what persisted (imperial control), what shifted (gains plus rising resistance), and why it mattered (unresolved tensions feeding the next war).

BeReady: 10-Second Takeaway

After World War I, the defeated empires' colonies did not become free — between the wars, Western and Japanese imperial states predominantly maintained control and in some cases gained more. The League of Nations mandate system transferred former German colonies to Britain and France (German East Africa to Britain, German South West Africa to South African control) and divided the post-Ottoman Middle East via Sykes-Picot — France over Syria and Lebanon, Britain over Iraq — drawing borders that ignored ethnic and sectarian lines. Japan gained by conquest, creating the puppet state of Manchukuo and the Greater East Asia Co-Prosperity Sphere. But empire was challenged: the Indian National Congress (galvanized by the 1919 Jallianwala Bagh massacre) and West African strikes and congresses against French rule pushed for self-determination. Continuity (control maintained) and change (territory gained, resistance rising) at once — the unresolved tensions that fuel Topic 7.6.
