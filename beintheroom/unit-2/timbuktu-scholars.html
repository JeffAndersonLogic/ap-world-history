The Scholars of Timbuktu

What the Routes Carry Besides Gold

Timbuktu, Mali Empire, c. 1355 CE AP Skill: CCOT LO-J · LO-K KC-3.1.III.D · KC-3.1.III.C · KC-3.3.II · KC-3.1.IV

You are Fatima al-Wazzani, senior scribe and intellectual advisor to the chief qadi of Sankore mosque-university. A delegation of scholars from Fez wants access to manuscripts that exist nowhere else in the world. Reports of a deadly plague moving along the trade routes have just reached the city. The qadi needs your counsel.

Step 1 — Enter the Room

Historical Situation

The library holds more than 700,000 manuscripts. You know this because you catalogued a third of them yourself.

They arrived the way everything arrives in Timbuktu: along the routes. A scholar fleeing Baghdad with a cartload of texts after the Mongol sack. A merchant from Morocco who traded a crate of astronomical treatises for a season's lodging. A pilgrim who carried a copy of Ibn Battuta's travel account — still being circulated in handwritten copies from Tangier to Cairo — and left it at the Sankore mosque because he had no room for it on the return journey.

Timbuktu became a city of books the same way it became a city of gold: because it sits at the intersection of routes that carry things from everywhere to everywhere else.

You are Fatima al-Wazzani, senior scribe and intellectual advisor to the chief qadi of Sankore. The qadi has asked you to prepare a recommendation for a question that has become urgent: a delegation of scholars has arrived from Fez, in Morocco, with a specific request. They want access to the library's collection of Greek scientific texts — works on astronomy, medicine, and mathematics that were translated into Arabic centuries ago and exist in Timbuktu in copies found nowhere else in the Islamic world. The Fez scholars want to copy them, take them north, and incorporate them into the curriculum of Morocco's own universities.

The case for sharing is obvious: this is what scholars do. Knowledge travels.

But the chief qadi has concerns you understand. Three of those manuscripts are the only known copies. If they leave Timbuktu — even as copies — what stops the originals from becoming irrelevant? And there is something else, something darker: word has reached Timbuktu from the north that a new sickness is moving along the trade routes from Central Asia toward the Mediterranean. Travelers who arrived last month from Egypt reported that entire cities are falling silent. The Fez delegation has been on the road for two months.

The routes bring books. The routes also bring what the books cannot cure.

What do you recommend to the qadi?

Step 2 — Your Role

Fatima al-Wazzani, Senior Scribe and Intellectual Advisor to the Chief Qadi of Sankore

Twenty years in the library Advisor to the qadi Cataloguer of the collection

You have spent twenty years in this library. You know which manuscripts are irreplaceable and which exist in dozens of copies elsewhere. You know the names of the scholars who brought each collection to Timbuktu and what they traded to get here. You have read Ibn Battuta's account of his travels — the man himself passed through Timbuktu — and you understand, perhaps better than anyone, what the intensification of exchange networks across Afro-Eurasia has produced: a world where ideas can travel from Baghdad to Fez to Timbuktu in a single generation.

You also know what else travels.

The qadi trusts your judgment on both the scholarly and the practical dimensions of this question. Your recommendation will shape Sankore's relationship with the Moroccan scholarly world for decades.

Your constraints:

  • The Fez scholars are legitimate — distinguished faculty from one of the great Islamic universities of North Africa. Insulting them has real diplomatic cost.
  • Three of the manuscripts in question are unique. If those originals are destroyed — fire, flood, theft — the copies going to Fez will be the only record of what they contained.
  • The plague reports are secondhand. You do not know with certainty how close it is. But the routes are faster than rumors.
  • Timbuktu's rise as an intellectual center was built on openness. The library has never turned away a legitimate scholar before.
Step 3 — The Decision

What Does Fatima Recommend to the Qadi?

Should the Sankore library share its unique manuscript collection with the Fez scholars — or restrict access to protect Timbuktu's intellectual standing and, perhaps, its physical safety?

A conditional middle path is acceptable — for example, allow copying but not access to the unique originals — but must be justified with evidence. Choose the position you can best defend.

Position A — Share the Manuscripts

The entire history of Timbuktu's rise as an intellectual center depends on the free movement of knowledge along trade routes. The Greek scientific texts arrived in Timbuktu because scholars carried them across borders. To refuse to share them is to deny the principle that made Sankore great.

Knowledge is not diminished by being copied — it multiplies. Sharing strengthens the Islamic scholarly world and confirms Timbuktu's role as its western anchor. The risk of plague is real but speculative; the opportunity to disseminate irreplaceable scholarship is concrete and present.

Evidence: KC-3.1.III.D · KC-3.1.III.C · KC-3.3.II

Position B — Restrict Access

The plague changes the calculation. The same routes that brought these manuscripts to Timbuktu are now carrying a pathogen that is reportedly emptying cities. The Fez scholars have been on the road for two months — they passed through regions where the sickness has been reported.

Admitting them to the library's inner collection, where scribes and scholars work in close quarters for weeks at a time, is a risk that Timbuktu cannot absorb. The manuscripts can be shared when the routes are safe again. The library's mission is to preserve knowledge for the Islamic world, not to endanger the city that houses it.

Evidence: KC-3.1.IV · KC-3.3.II · KC-3.1.III.D

Step 4 — Read the Evidence

Primary Source Documents

Read all four documents. Your written report must cite evidence from at least two. Strong arguments name the source and explain why it supports your position — not just what it says.

Document 1

Ibn Battuta's Account of Timbuktu, c. 1352

"I arrived in Timbuktu and found it a city of considerable size, the seat of a great qadi and scholars of distinction. The markets are excellent and the merchants numerous. The inhabitants are of an easy disposition and skilled in trade. Books are sold here at good prices — more profitable than any other goods. The city draws scholars from Morocco, Egypt, and the lands beyond the eastern sea. I have not seen a place in all my travels where learning and commerce are so entirely one thing."
— From Ibn Battuta's Rihla (Travels), circulated in manuscript form across the Islamic world
AP Connection: KC-3.1.III.C — Ibn Battuta as an example of the increased travel writing that intensified exchange networks produced; KC-3.3.II — Timbuktu as an urban center buoyed by trade and knowledge exchange.
Document 2

Letter from the Fez Delegation, Chief Scholar Ahmad ibn Khaldun al-Fassi

"Honored Qadi, we arrive from the Qarawiyyin University in Fez with a single purpose. We have learned through our own scholars that Sankore holds three texts of Ptolemy and one of Galen translated into Arabic by scholars of Baghdad — texts that were thought lost when the Mongols burned the House of Wisdom in 1258. If these texts exist in Timbuktu, they must be shared with the Islamic world. We ask only for the right to copy them. We will work in whatever space you designate, for as long as you require. The knowledge belongs to all of us."
— Delivered to the Sankore qadi's office upon the delegation's arrival, c. 1355
AP Connection: KC-3.1.III.D — diffusion of scientific and intellectual traditions across exchange networks; KC-3.1.III.C — scholars as a category of Afro-Eurasian traveler who intensified cultural exchange.
Document 3

Report from a Timbuktu Merchant Returning from Egypt, c. 1354

"I returned from Cairo three weeks ago by the northern route. In Egypt, the markets of Alexandria are quieter than I have ever seen them. Merchants from the Genoese quarter told me of a sickness spreading from the east — they call it the Black Death. It travels with the caravans. The rats, they say. I saw three trading vessels quarantined in the harbor at Tunis. I do not know how close it is to the Moroccan routes, but the Fez road passes through regions I would not travel today."
— Testimony recorded in the Sankore administrative files
AP Connection: KC-3.1.IV — diffusion of pathogens along trade routes; bubonic plague as a consequence of the same connectivity that spread culture and knowledge.
Document 4

Sankore Library Catalogue Entry, c. 1340

"Current holdings include: 14,000 volumes in Arabic on law, theology, and Quranic interpretation; 3,200 volumes on history and geography; 890 volumes on mathematics, astronomy, and medicine, including three texts of Greek origin translated in Baghdad and not known to exist in any other library west of Cairo. These Greek texts were acquired through the estate of a scholar who fled Baghdad following the Mongol conquest of 1258 and died in Timbuktu in 1271. No copies exist outside this library."
— From the administrative records of the Sankore manuscript collection
AP Connection: KC-3.1.III.D — knowledge diffusion as both a product of connectivity and a thing connectivity can threaten; KC-3.3.II — Timbuktu's rise as an intellectual urban center tied to the expansion of trade networks.
Step 5 — Write Your Report

Your Task: Advise the Qadi

The qadi needs your recommendation before the Fez scholars grow impatient. Write your advisory response.

Your response should:

  1. State your position clearly — share the manuscripts, or restrict access? (A conditional middle path is acceptable but must be justified with evidence.)
  2. Explain what changed as Afro-Eurasian trade networks intensified after 1200. What is new about the intellectual and cultural world Timbuktu inhabits?
  3. Explain what stayed the same — what continuities remain even as the routes changed everything?
  4. Use specific evidence from at least two documents.
  5. Acknowledge the tension — the same routes that built Timbuktu's library may now threaten it. How does that tension shape your recommendation?
Target length: 150–250 words • You have read Ibn Battuta. You understand the world these routes have built. Write like someone who knows both what was gained and what can be lost.
