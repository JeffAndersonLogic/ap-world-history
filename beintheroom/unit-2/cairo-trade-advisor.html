The Trade Advisor of Cairo

Every Route in the World Passes Through Here

Cairo, Mamluk Sultanate, c. 1340 CE AP Skill: Comparison LO-L KC-3.1 · KC-3.1.I.A.i · KC-3.1.I.C.i · KC-3.3.I.B

You are Layla bint Rashid al-Misri, senior trade advisor to the Mamluk Sultan. Cairo sits at the intersection of the Silk Roads, the Indian Ocean network, and the Trans-Saharan routes. The sultan wants a major investment in one of the three — and he wants it justified by a genuine comparison, not just a preference. You have until morning.

Step 1 — Enter the Room

Historical Situation

Cairo does not belong to any single trade network. It belongs to all of them.

To the east, the Red Sea connects the city to the Indian Ocean — the spice ships from Calicut, the dhows from the Swahili Coast, the vessels from Gujarat carrying cotton and indigo. To the northeast, the overland routes through the Levant connect Cairo to Damascus, Baghdad, and the arteries of the Silk Roads that run all the way to China. And to the south, the Nile road leads to the Saharan crossing points where the caravans from Mali and Ghana arrive with gold dust, ivory, and salt.

Sit in the right cafe in the Khan el-Khalili market and you can hear, in a single afternoon, Swahili traders negotiating for Egyptian linen, Moroccan merchants arranging bills of exchange in the banking houses, and Genoese factors haggling over spice prices in the warehouse district near the docks. Cairo is not just a city. It is the point where the world talks to itself.

You are Layla bint Rashid al-Misri, senior trade advisor to Sultan al-Nasir Muhammad ibn Qalawun — the most powerful ruler in the Islamic world and one of the wealthiest men alive. The sultan has summoned you for a specific purpose.

The Mamluk treasury is strong, but not unlimited. The sultan has decided to make a significant investment in trade infrastructure — caravanserai improvements, harbor upgrades, diplomatic missions to trading partners, and expanded warehousing. He can fund one major push. He wants your recommendation on which network to prioritize: the overland Silk Roads connections through the Levant, the Indian Ocean routes via the Red Sea, or the Trans-Saharan routes via the Nile and the Saharan crossings.

He has been told that all three are important. He wants to know which one is most important — and why.

You have until tomorrow morning.

Step 2 — Your Role

Layla bint Rashid al-Misri, Senior Trade Advisor to Sultan al-Nasir Muhammad ibn Qalawun

Thirty years in the trade offices Correspondent across all three networks Unit 2 capstone perspective

You have spent thirty years in the trade offices of Cairo. You have corresponded with merchants in Quanzhou, Calicut, Timbuktu, Venice, and Samarkand. You have read Ibn Battuta's account of the Indian Ocean — he passed through Cairo twice. You have studied the commercial innovations of the Genoese bankers who operate in Alexandria. And you have personally negotiated caravan agreements with Malian gold merchants at the southern waypoints.

You understand something that most advisors miss: the three networks are not the same thing in different geography. They differ in what they carry, how they carry it, who controls them, what makes them vulnerable, and what kind of infrastructure makes them grow. A recommendation that treats them as interchangeable is a recommendation that will waste the sultan's investment.

Your constraints:

  • The sultan is asking for a priority, not a ranking. He wants one major investment, not three medium ones.
  • The Mamluk Sultanate's strength is built partly on its position as the broker between East and West. Whatever you recommend must protect — or extend — that position.
  • You are aware of troubling reports from the east: word of a sickness moving along the Central Asian trade routes. You do not know what it means yet, but you are factoring it into your assessment of the overland Silk Roads' stability.
  • Your recommendation will be public. Other advisors will challenge it. You need to be able to name not just your preferred network's strengths but also the differences that make it preferable to the alternatives.
Step 3 — The Decision

Which Network Should the Mamluk Sultanate Prioritize?

Choose one network for major infrastructure investment — and be ready to justify that choice by naming what makes the networks different from each other. All three positions are historically defensible. The comparison skill is demonstrated in whether you can use the differences as the basis of your argument.

Position A — Indian Ocean

Prioritize the Red Sea / Indian Ocean Routes

The Indian Ocean is the fastest-growing trade network in Afro-Eurasia and the one most dependent on port infrastructure that Cairo can directly control. The Red Sea gives Egypt privileged access to the spice trade — the most valuable commodity in the Mediterranean world.

Unlike the Silk Roads, which require political stability across multiple empires Cairo cannot influence, the Indian Ocean depends primarily on maritime technology and harbor relationships the Mamluk Sultanate can invest in directly. The network also carries bulk commodities alongside luxury goods, making it more resilient than the overland routes.

Evidence: KC-3.1.I.A.ii · KC-3.1.I.C.ii · KC-3.1.I.A.iii
Position B — Silk Roads

Prioritize the Silk Roads / Levant Connections

The Silk Roads deliver what Cairo's luxury market and European trading partners most want: Chinese silk, porcelain, paper, and the sophisticated banking innovations — bills of exchange, credit houses — that are the most advanced commercial tools in the world.

Pax Mongolica has made the overland routes more stable than they have been in decades; this is the moment to invest. Moreover, the Silk Roads bring Persian and Chinese merchants into Cairo's orbit, deepening the cultural and intellectual ties that make Cairo a world city.

Evidence: KC-3.1.I.A.i · KC-3.1.I.C.i · KC-3.3.I.B
Position C — Trans-Saharan

Prioritize the Trans-Saharan Routes

The Trans-Saharan routes bring the one commodity that underpins every other network: gold. Mali's gold funds Mediterranean commerce, North African mints, and the credit systems that make long-distance trade in all three networks possible. Cairo's control of the Nile corridor makes it the natural broker for Trans-Saharan goods entering the Mediterranean.

Investing in the southern routes means investing in the raw material that everyone else's trade depends on — a strategic leverage point that neither the Indian Ocean nor the Silk Roads can match.

Evidence: KC-3.1.I.A.iv · KC-3.1.I.E.ii · KC-3.1.II.A.ii

Quick Comparison Reference — How the Networks Differ

Silk Roads
  • Carries: silk, porcelain, paper, luxury goods
  • Technology: caravanserai, overland camel routes
  • Vulnerability: political stability across multiple states
  • Commercial innovation: bills of exchange, banking
  • Key nodes: Samarkand, Tabriz, Damascus, Baghdad
Indian Ocean
  • Carries: spices, cotton, ivory, bulk goods
  • Technology: dhows, compass, monsoon navigation
  • Vulnerability: seasonal monsoon windows, storms
  • Commercial innovation: diasporic merchant communities
  • Key nodes: Calicut, Kilwa, Hormuz, Aden, Gujarat
Trans-Saharan
  • Carries: gold, salt, ivory, enslaved persons
  • Technology: improved camel saddle, caravanserai
  • Vulnerability: desert conditions, Tuareg middlemen
  • Commercial innovation: imperial control (Mali)
  • Key nodes: Timbuktu, Taghaza, Cairo (northern terminus)
Step 4 — Read the Evidence

Primary Source Documents

Read all four documents. Your advisory brief must cite evidence from at least two. Each document anchors a different aspect of the network comparison — use them to support your comparative argument, not just your preferred network.

Document 1

Report from the Cairo Custom House, c. 1338

"Goods entering Cairo via the Red Sea (Indian Ocean routes): pepper, cinnamon, ginger, indigo, cotton cloth, teak timber, and pearls. Principal origin points: Calicut, Quilon, Hormuz, Aden, and the Swahili Coast. Volume this year: highest recorded in a decade, attributed to improved navigation and expanded Gujarati merchant operations. Goods entering via the Levant (Silk Roads connections): silk, porcelain, paper, and metalwork. Principal origin points: Damascus, Baghdad, Tabriz, and, via overland relay, Samarkand and points east. Volume this year: moderate; Mongol succession disputes have created delays at several waypoints. Goods entering via the Nile and southern routes (Trans-Saharan connections): gold dust, ivory, enslaved persons, and kola nuts. Principal origin points: Mali, Ghana region, and the Saharan crossing towns. Volume this year: increasing, as Mali's expansion has drawn new suppliers into the network."
— Annual summary of goods processed through Cairo's trade offices, compiled for the sultan's treasury
AP Connection: KC-3.1 / KC-3.3.I.B — the demand for luxury goods across all three networks; the structural difference in what each network carries and where goods originate.
Document 2

Letter from a Genoese Factor in Alexandria, c. 1335

"To my partners in Genoa. The spice trade through Alexandria is the most valuable commerce in the Mediterranean. A single shipment of pepper from the Red Sea route is worth more than three seasons of Silk Road porcelain — not because the porcelain is less valuable, but because the Red Sea route is faster and more reliable than anything moving overland. The Silk Roads depend on political stability across five different powers; the Indian Ocean depends on knowing the monsoon. We know the monsoon. I would advise any investor to follow the spices."
— Commercial correspondence, referenced in Cairo trade office records
AP Connection: KC-3.1.I.A.ii / KC-3.1.I.C.ii — the Indian Ocean's reliance on environmental knowledge and maritime technology as distinguishing features; the contrast with overland route dependence on political stability.
Document 3

Account of a Malian Gold Merchant, Cairo, c. 1336

"I have crossed the Sahara fourteen times. The routes are difficult — no one should pretend otherwise. But they are reliable in a way that sea routes are not. When the wind fails or a storm scatters a fleet, those merchants lose everything at once. When a camel dies, a caravan loses one camel's worth of cargo. The desert route from Timbuktu to Cairo takes three months; a sea route from the Swahili Coast to the Red Sea takes two. But I can guarantee my gold will arrive. Can you say the same of a ship from Calicut in the wrong season?"
— Testimony recorded in the Cairo trade advisor's files following a commercial negotiation
AP Connection: KC-3.1.I.A.iv / KC-3.1.II.A.ii — the Trans-Saharan routes' dependence on camel technology and overland infrastructure rather than environmental navigation; the comparative reliability structure of each network.
Document 4

Ibn Battuta's Observation of Cairo, c. 1326

"Cairo is the mother of cities and the seat of Pharaoh the tyrant. She is mistress of broad provinces and fruitful lands, boundless in multitude of buildings, peerless in beauty and splendor. I arrived on my way to Mecca and found that I could purchase in the Khan el-Khalili market: silk from China, spices from India, gold from Mali, and linen from the Egyptian countryside. All the world, it seemed, had decided to trade in one place. The banking houses here use letters of credit — a sheet of paper in Cairo becomes gold in Aleppo — that are more reliable than carrying coin across any route."
— From Ibn Battuta's Rihla (Travels), widely circulated in manuscript form
AP Connection: KC-3.1.I.C.i — bills of exchange and banking houses as shared commercial innovations present across networks; KC-3.1 — Cairo as a node where all three networks converge, making both their similarities and differences visible.
Step 5 — Advise the Sultan

Your Task: Advisory Brief to the Sultan

The sultan needs your recommendation by morning. Write your advisory brief. The sultan does not want a summary of all three networks — he wants a comparative argument: a recommendation that is grounded in the differences you have identified.

Your response must:

  1. State your recommendation clearly — which network should the Mamluk Sultanate prioritize, and why?
  2. Identify at least one similarity among the three trade networks. What do the Silk Roads, the Indian Ocean, and the Trans-Saharan routes have in common?
  3. Identify at least two differences among the networks — in what they carry, how they operate, what makes them grow, or what makes them vulnerable.
  4. Use specific evidence from at least two documents.
  5. Acknowledge what you are giving up — if you prioritize one network, what risks or opportunities are you deprioritizing?
Target length: 150–250 words • Start with the comparison. Build to the recommendation. If you cannot name what makes the networks different, you cannot defend a priority.
