The Salt Road at Taghaza

Nothing Crosses the Sahara Without Permission

Mali Empire, c. 1330 CE AP Skill: Causation LO-H · LO-I KC-3.1.II.A.ii · KC-3.1.I.A.iv · KC-3.1.I.E.ii

You are Kofi Diabaté, Regional Governor of Mali's northern trade routes, riding into Taghaza to advise the emperor. Mansa Musa is waiting. The salt is waiting. The decision cannot wait much longer.

Step 1 — Enter the Room

Historical Situation

The salt blocks are cut in slabs the size of a door and stacked in the shade of the mine walls while men who have never seen a river pour water rationed from goatskins and wait for the caravan to form.

Taghaza is not a pleasant place. The houses are built of salt. The mosque is built of salt. The bread comes from a thousand miles away. But the salt itself — white, crystalline, dense — is worth its weight in gold in the markets of the south. Literally. In Timbuktu, the exchange rate is one slab of Taghaza salt for one measure of gold dust. The empire runs on this transaction.

You are Kofi Diabaté, regional governor of Mali's northern trade routes, and you have ridden three days from Timbuktu to be here. Mansa Musa sent you — the emperor who has made Mali the envy of the known world, whose pilgrimage to Mecca two years ago reportedly distributed so much gold in Cairo that the Egyptian gold market is still recovering. He wants a report and a recommendation.

The problem is a proposal that has been sitting on your desk since last month. A Tuareg caravan master named Ag Amayas controls three of the largest camel trains operating between Taghaza and the Niger River. He has come to you with a petition: Mali should fund the construction of two new caravanserai stations — rest stops with water, shade, and guards — on the stretches of the route that currently have none. The investment would reduce caravan losses by an estimated third and bring more caravans through the Malian-controlled corridor.

But the treasurer in Timbuktu has raised a counterargument: the existing waypoints are already profitable and the marginal return on deep-desert infrastructure is uncertain. That money could be spent expanding the markets at Timbuktu itself — deepening the city's role as a hub rather than extending the tentacles of the route.

Mansa Musa is watching. The trade is watching. The Tuareg caravan masters, who could choose routes that bypass Malian waypoints entirely, are watching.

What do you recommend?

Step 2 — Your Role

Kofi Diabaté, Regional Governor of Mali's Northern Trade Routes

Appointed by Mansa Musa Authority over northern corridors Reports directly to the Emperor

You were appointed by Mansa Musa himself — the man whose pilgrimage to Mecca in 1324–25 reportedly caused inflation in Egypt's gold markets by flooding Cairo with gold gifts. You have traveled the route between Taghaza and Timbuktu more times than you can count. You know the desert's rhythms: where the wells are reliable, where the sands shift, which Tuareg clans are trustworthy and which are not.

Your job is to keep the gold and salt flowing. Mali's treasury, its military, and its reputation all depend on the Trans-Saharan routes remaining in Malian hands.

Your constraints:

  • Mansa Musa needs a recommendation, not a delay. He is building his legacy and he wants infrastructure that matches his reputation.
  • The Tuareg caravan masters are not subjects of Mali — they are contractors. If the route through Malian waypoints becomes more dangerous or less profitable than alternatives, they will find another path.
  • The Timbuktu treasurer's objection is not wrong: the city's scholars, markets, and banking infrastructure generate enormous soft power. Timbuktu is already changing the culture of West Africa.
  • The camel saddle innovation that made long-distance desert trade viable is less than a century old in its improved form. The infrastructure of the routes is still being built. You are in the middle of creating something that does not yet have a final shape.
Step 3 — The Decision

What Does Governor Diabaté Recommend?

Should you recommend that Mali invest in extending caravanserai infrastructure into the deep desert — or concentrate resources on developing Timbuktu as a deeper trade hub?

Each position has real historical logic. Choose the one you can best defend with evidence from the documents below.

Position A — Extend the Caravanserai Infrastructure

The Trans-Saharan routes only work because of infrastructure — caravanserai, water sources, guards, and reliable waypoints. Every stretch of desert that lacks a caravanserai is a risk that drives merchants toward alternative routes. Mali's power depends on being indispensable to the physical movement of goods.

Ag Amayas estimates caravan losses could drop by a third. Fewer losses means more goods arriving, more gold flowing north, more volume at Timbuktu. The investment extends the empire's reach and draws new merchants into Mali's economic orbit.

Evidence: KC-3.1.II.A.ii · KC-3.1.I.A.iv · KC-3.1.I.E.ii

Position B — Concentrate Investment at Timbuktu

Timbuktu is already the greatest city on the Trans-Saharan routes. It functions as a market, a religious center, and a banking hub. The value of Timbuktu is not just in moving goods through it — it is in what happens when goods arrive.

Deep-desert caravanserai is expensive to build, expensive to maintain, and always vulnerable to raids and sandstorms. Concentrate the empire's wealth where it is already compounding: in the city.

Evidence: KC-3.1.I.E.ii · KC-3.1.I.A.iv

Step 4 — Read the Evidence

Primary Source Documents

Read all four documents. Your written report must cite evidence from at least two. Strong arguments name the source and explain why it supports your position — not just what it says.

Document 1

Report from Ag Amayas, Tuareg Caravan Master

"Honored Governor. I speak for the caravan masters who move salt, gold, ivory, and cloth across the northern routes. In the past two seasons, we have lost eleven camels and four men on the stretch between the third waypoint and Taghaza — a distance of nine days with no water or shelter. The new camel saddle design allows for heavier loads, but heavier loads require more reliable staging points. If Mali builds two stations on this stretch, I estimate losses will fall by a third and volume will increase accordingly. Without them, I cannot guarantee my caravans will continue to use this corridor."
— Delivered to the governor's office, Timbuktu, c. 1330
AP Connection: KC-3.1.II.A.ii — innovation in transportation technology (improved camel saddle) requiring complementary infrastructure; KC-3.1.I.A.iv — improved commercial practices expanding trade route range.
Document 2

Letter from the Timbuktu Treasury

"Governor, the proposed caravanserai investment is estimated at 4,000 gold mithqals for construction and 800 per year for maintenance and guards. We note that the same investment, directed at expanding Timbuktu's western market district, would accommodate forty additional merchant stalls, two additional caravanserai lodges within the city, and a new warehouse block. Timbuktu's position as the terminus of the northern route is the source of our advantage — not the desert itself. We recommend prioritizing depth over extension."
— Sent to Governor Diabaté in advance of the Taghaza inspection
AP Connection: KC-3.1.I.E.ii — Mali's urban centers as the mechanism by which imperial expansion draws new merchants into trade networks.
Document 3

Account of a Moroccan Traveler, c. 1320

"The people of Mali have made the road from the north more reliable than it was even thirty years ago. There are stations now where a caravan may rest, water its animals, and trade small goods. The governor's agents collect a toll at each station — modest, but consistent. I met merchants from Morocco, Egypt, and even one man who claimed to have come from a land beyond the eastern sea. All of them passed through Timbuktu. The city feeds on the route as a river feeds on its tributaries."
— Excerpt from a travel narrative, cited in later Timbuktu scholarship
AP Connection: KC-3.1.I.E.ii — Mali's imperial expansion drawing new people into Afro-Eurasian trade networks; KC-3.1.I.A.iv — expanded commercial practices increasing trade volume.
Document 4

Imperial Decree on Trade Route Security, Mansa Musa, c. 1325

"Let it be known that the trade routes of Mali are the veins of the empire. Any caravan operating within Mali's territory travels under the emperor's protection. Any raid upon a caravan is a raid upon the emperor himself. The routes shall be extended, maintained, and defended at the emperor's expense, that all merchants — Malian, Moroccan, Egyptian, and beyond — shall know that Mali's roads are safe."
— Referenced in court records, Timbuktu, following the emperor's return from pilgrimage
AP Connection: KC-3.1.I.E.ii — explicit statement of imperial investment in trade infrastructure as a mechanism of Afro-Eurasian connectivity; KC-3.1.I.A.iv — imperial commercial practices expanding trade network reach.
Step 5 — Write Your Report

Your Task: Report to the Emperor

Mansa Musa is waiting for your recommendation. Write your report to the emperor.

Your response should:

  1. State your position clearly — extend the deep-desert caravanserai infrastructure, or concentrate investment at Timbuktu?
  2. Explain at least two causes of Trans-Saharan trade growth that make this decision significant. What forces made the desert crossable — and what forces keep it profitable?
  3. Identify at least one effect of Trans-Saharan trade growth on Mali or on Afro-Eurasian connections. What has this network already produced?
  4. Use specific evidence from at least two documents.
  5. Acknowledge the tradeoff — what does Mali give up with your recommendation?
Target length: 150–250 words • Remember: you are a Malian governor writing to the most powerful ruler in West Africa. Be direct. Be specific. The emperor has already seen the world — he does not need to be flattered.
