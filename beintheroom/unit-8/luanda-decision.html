The Luanda Decision

Luanda, Angola, November 1975. Portugal is withdrawing. Independence Day is days away. South African forces are advancing from the south. FNLA columns are pressing from the north. Three liberation movements each claim the right to govern Angola, and both superpowers are extending offers. You are a senior advisor within the MPLA leadership circle. The question is not whether to fight. The question is who you fight with, and what you owe them afterward.

The Situation Before You

The year is November 1975. Angola has been a Portuguese colony for five centuries. Portugal's Carnation Revolution in April 1974 collapsed the Salazar dictatorship and ended Portugal's will to hold its African empire. The Alvor Agreement signed in January 1975 set November 11, 1975 as Angola's independence date and called for a transitional government shared among the three liberation movements: the MPLA, the FNLA, and UNITA.

That agreement collapsed months ago. The MPLA, Marxist, Soviet-aligned, strongest in Luanda and the coast, controls the capital. The FNLA, backed by Zaire and the United States, presses from the north. UNITA, increasingly backed by the United States and apartheid South Africa, holds the southern interior. South African regular army units crossed the border weeks ago, advancing toward Luanda with UNITA forces. The MPLA faces a war it may not win on its own.

Cuba has offered combat troops and Soviet arms. The United States, which previously backed the MPLA before switching to its opponents, has offered quiet channels for recognition and possible aid through intermediaries. The Organisation of African Unity has called for an African solution to an African problem. Independence Day is approaching. You have three decisions to make, and no perfect answer to any of them.

Note: You are reasoning from November 1975. You know what is happening. You do not know what the Angolan Civil War will become, that it will last until 2002, that it will kill hundreds of thousands, that the Cold War itself will end before the fighting does.

Decision (a): The Cuban Offer

Accept Cuban combat troops and Soviet arms shipments to hold Luanda and push back the South African advance, or refuse foreign troops to preserve the MPLA's claim to independent Angolan governance?

Decision (b): The Western Channel

Pursue quiet talks with U.S. intermediaries for recognition and possible economic support, or denounce U.S. involvement given its alignment with South Africa and continued funding of UNITA?

Decision (c): The African Path

Appeal to the Organisation of African Unity and the Bandung-era principles of African solidarity for an African-led solution, accepting that OAU mediation may arrive too slowly to stop the columns advancing on the capital?

The Situation Before You

You are advising the MPLA leadership on all three decisions. But your most important recommendation concerns the fundamental question: what kind of sovereignty are you fighting for? Choose the path you believe Angola should take as independence approaches.

Your answer to this question will shape how you approach all three decisions, the Cuban offer, the Western channel, and the African path.

Accept Cuban Intervention and Soviet Arms

Accept Cuban combat troops and the Soviet arms pipeline to hold Luanda and survive independence. South Africa's military threat is immediate, principles cannot stop advancing columns. Deal with the dependency question after Angola is secured.

Pursue Quiet Western Recognition

Open back-channel talks with U.S. intermediaries for diplomatic recognition and economic aid, while maintaining military positions in Luanda. The U.S. has strategic interests in Angolan oil. Recognition may be achievable if the MPLA can credibly claim the government.

Appeal to the OAU and African Unity

Formally request OAU mediation and invoke the Bandung-era principles of non-interference and African solidarity. An African solution, however imperfect, avoids the Cold War dependency trap. The cost is time Angola may not have.

Negotiate Angolan Power-Sharing

Reopen talks with UNITA for a coalition government before independence, without superpower preconditions. Angola's civil war will be more costly than any Cold War dependency. A negotiated settlement preserves Angolan sovereignty, if UNITA and its backers will accept it.

Step 6

Step Out of the Room: AP Reflection

Now leave the role and write as a historian. Use what you experienced in this scenario to explain broader patterns about how superpowers maintained influence through proxy wars, and what that experience meant for postcolonial states caught between them.

AP Reflection Prompt
Compare the ways in which the United States and the Soviet Union sought to maintain influence in Angola. In your response, use at least two specific historical details from the scenario, including at least one reference to the methods each superpower used (arms, advisors, local clients, recognition, framing), and connect them to the broader pattern of proxy war competition in postcolonial states. Then explain: what does this scenario reveal about what proxy war competition cost the postcolonial states where it was fought? (Continuity hook: The advisor in this room remembers the Bandung Conference of 1955, where Sukarno argued that newly independent nations should refuse both superpower agendas.
