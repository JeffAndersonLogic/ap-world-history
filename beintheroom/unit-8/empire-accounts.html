The Empire Accounts

London, February 1947. Britain's treasury is nearly empty. Three simultaneous crises demand decisions the empire can no longer afford to make alone. Across the Atlantic, Washington is watching, and across Europe, Moscow is advancing. The world that Britain built over two centuries is presenting its bill. You are a senior advisor to Prime Minister Clement Attlee's cabinet. What do you recommend?

Historical Situation

You are entering the most consequential few months in modern British history. In February 1947, the Attlee government faces a reality it can no longer avoid: the empire that once commanded a quarter of the world's surface cannot be maintained. The treasury, drained by six years of total war and burdened with debt to the United States, has run out of the resources that make global power possible.

Three crises have landed on the cabinet's desk at the same time. Each is urgent. Each is connected. Each decision will be shaped by the same underlying reality: the United States and the Soviet Union, not Britain, now set the terms of the global order.

India

The Indian National Congress, led by Nehru and supported by Gandhi's decades of mass organizing, demands a firm date for the transfer of power. The Muslim League, led by Jinnah, insists on a separate state for Muslims. Britain must decide: announce independence, or attempt to hold on.

Greece & Turkey

A communist insurgency in Greece threatens to bring a Soviet-aligned government to power in the Mediterranean. Britain has been funding Greek and Turkish resistance, but the treasury is exhausted. Britain must decide: continue the aid, or notify Washington that it must withdraw.

Palestine

Britain holds the Mandate for Palestine, where Jewish and Arab communities are in violent conflict. Jewish paramilitary groups are attacking British forces. Arab states are threatening war. Britain must decide: continue administering the Mandate, or refer it to the United Nations.

Step 2

The Situation Before You

Three crises, one principle. Britain cannot maintain its current commitments, but how it withdraws matters enormously. Each approach has different costs and implications for Britain's position in the world, for the people in the territories involved, and for the emerging rivalry between the United States and the Soviet Union.

Which principle should guide your recommendations to Prime Minister Attlee across all three crises?

Hold the Imperial Line

Maintain British presence in all three theaters. Britain's global position depends on appearing strong. Withdrawal signals weakness that rivals will exploit.

Negotiate Firm Transitions

Set firm dates and terms for British withdrawal from each crisis. Work with local leaders, Nehru, Greek democrats, to manage the transition and retain British influence where possible.

Defer to Washington

Notify the United States that Britain must withdraw, particularly from Greece and Turkey. This invites American leadership but also American conditions. Britain trades imperial control for American support.

Refer to International Bodies

Hand the crises to the United Nations and multilateral institutions. This distributes responsibility and provides political cover, but surrenders British control and risks UN paralysis.
