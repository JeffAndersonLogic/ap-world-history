# BeInTheRoom Scenario Blueprint

BeInTheRoom activities are AP World History roleplay scenarios built around historically grounded decision points. They are not simple character simulations. Each activity should help students understand power, perspective, evidence, tradeoffs, and historical reasoning.

## Core Purpose

Students should enter a historical problem, adopt a role, make a defensible recommendation, defend that recommendation with evidence, receive AI coaching, and then step out of character to write an AP-style reflection.

Recommended classroom flow:

1. Enter the Room
2. Choose a Historical Perspective
3. Choose a Policy or Decision
4. Select Evidence
5. Build a Position
6. Use the MagicSchool AI Coach
7. Step Out of the Room with an AP Reflection

## Scenario Requirements

Each scenario should include the following components.

### 1. Scenario Title

Use an action-oriented title.

Examples:
- Advising the Song Court
- Defending Mali's Reputation
- Governing After Abbasid Fragmentation
- Holding Medieval Europe Together
- Debating Power in the Mexica World

### 2. AP Topic Connection

Include:
- Unit
- Topic
- Learning objective
- Historical reasoning skill
- Key concepts or illustrative examples

### 3. Historical Situation

The situation should answer:
- Where are we?
- What historical moment are students entering?
- What tension is unfolding?
- Why does the decision matter?
- What AP theme does the scenario reveal?

A strong situation places students inside a historical system where every option has benefits, costs, supporters, and critics.

### 4. Central Dilemma

The dilemma must force tradeoffs. Avoid yes/no questions.

Good dilemma stems:
- What should the state prioritize?
- How should a ruler respond to competing pressures?
- Which network, institution, or belief system should receive support?
- How should power be defended, justified, or expanded?

### 5. Historical Roles

Each role should include:
- Name
- Social position/status
- Power and limitations
- Goals
- Fears
- Historical lens
- Likely policy preference
- Blind spot or tension

Roles should represent different positions in the historical system, not just famous individuals.

### 6. Policy or Decision Options

Each scenario should include 3-4 options.

Each option should include:
- Title
- What the policy does
- Historical evidence connected to it
- Who benefits
- Who worries
- Possible tradeoff

### 7. Evidence Bank

Each activity should provide 8-12 evidence chips.

Each evidence chip should include:
- Evidence title
- Short explanation
- Why it matters
- Which roles/policies it might support

Students should select at least two evidence chips before writing.

### 8. Position Builder

Students should produce:
- Role
- Policy choice
- Evidence selected
- First draft argument
- Tradeoff or risk
- Opposing role or viewpoint

### 9. MagicSchool AI Coach Bridge

The page should generate a copy-ready prompt that includes:
- Scenario title
- Central dilemma
- Student role
- Role goals/fears/power
- Policy choice
- Evidence selected
- Tradeoff/risk
- Opposing viewpoint
- Student first draft

The chatbot should ask one question at a time and should not write the final answer for the student.

## Master MagicSchool Chatbot Prompt

Use this as the base prompt for a reusable MagicSchool chatbot.

```text
You are the BeInTheRoom Historical Thinking Coach for an AP World History student.

Your job is not to write the student's answer. Your job is to hold a meaningful coaching dialogue that helps the student better understand the historical role they selected, the position they are defending, the evidence they are using, and the tradeoffs in the scenario.

Ask one question at a time.

Always begin by asking the student to paste:
1. the scenario title,
2. their historical role,
3. their policy choice or decision,
4. their evidence,
5. their first draft argument.

After the student responds, guide them through these stages:

Stage 1: Role Understanding
Ask what their role wants, fears, values, and has power to influence.

Stage 2: Evidence Check
Ask what specific historical evidence supports their position. Push for AP World vocabulary and concrete examples.

Stage 3: Tradeoff
Ask what is risky, costly, or incomplete about their chosen policy.

Stage 4: Opposing Perspective
Ask which other role would disagree and why.

Stage 5: Historical Thinking
Ask whether their argument uses causation, comparison, continuity and change, or contextualization.

Stage 6: Revision
Ask the student to revise their argument in 4-6 sentences using role, evidence, tradeoff, and reasoning.

Rules:
- Do not write the final answer for the student.
- Do not invent evidence outside the scenario unless clearly labeled as background.
- Keep questions short and student-friendly.
- Push the student to explain because, not just name facts.
- If the student gives a weak answer, ask a follow-up question instead of correcting everything at once.
- End by asking the student to step out of character and explain what the scenario reveals about the AP World topic.
```

## Final AP Reflection

Every BeInTheRoom should end with an out-of-character AP reflection.

Suggested structure:

1. What did your role want and fear?
2. What evidence best supported your position?
3. What tradeoff complicated your recommendation?
4. What did an opposing role reveal?
5. What does this scenario reveal about the AP World topic?

## Quality Rule

A BeInTheRoom scenario is ready only when:
- the dilemma has no perfect answer,
- roles have real historical interests and limitations,
- students must use evidence,
- students must consider tradeoffs,
- students must respond to an opposing viewpoint,
- AI coaching deepens thinking rather than replacing student work,
- the final product connects back to AP historical reasoning.
