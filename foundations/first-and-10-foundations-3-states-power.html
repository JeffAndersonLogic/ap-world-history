First & 10 Reading

Four Ways to Hold Power

Every state faces the same problem: how do a few people hold power over many, across great distances, and make it last? Between roughly 550 BCE and 220 CE, four classical civilizations, Persia, Han China, Greece, and Rome, each answered it in a strikingly different way. Comparing their answers is how you learn to analyze any state in AP World.

Causation Continuity & Change Argumentation

Before You Read

For each civilization, track two things: the specific tool it used to hold power, and the limit of that tool, who was left out, or where resistance appeared. Strong historians see both how power was built and where it cracked.

Reading Target

By the end, you should be able to explain how Persia, Han China, Greece, and Rome each solved the problem of organizing power, and compare at least two of their approaches using specific evidence.

1

Contextualization

The Problem Every State Must Solve

A state is an organized political community with authority over a territory and the people in it. Every state, ancient or modern, must solve the same four problems: it must extract resources (taxes, labor, and soldiers), defend its territory, administer people spread across distance, and make its rule feel legitimate rather than merely forced.

Between about 550 BCE and 220 CE, four classical civilizations became famous for how they answered these problems, the Persian Empire, Han China, the Greek city-states, and Rome. Their solutions were remarkably different: one ruled by tolerating difference, one by centralized bureaucracy, one by citizen self-government, and one by law and shared citizenship.

AP Thinking, Contextualization

The states AP World studies from c.1200 did not invent government from scratch. They inherited models worked out by these classical states. Learn the four problems, extraction, defense, administration, legitimacy, and you have a checklist you can run on any state in the course.

2

Persia

Persia: An Empire Ruled by Tolerance

Founded by Cyrus the Great around 550 BCE, the Persian (Achaemenid) Empire was the largest the world had yet seen, stretching from the Indus River to the Aegean Sea. Rather than crush the many peoples it conquered, Persia ruled through tolerance and delegation. It divided the empire into provinces called satrapies, each run by a governor, a satrap, who collected tribute and kept order, while local languages, customs, and religions were largely left alone.

To hold this vast space together, Persia built the Royal Road, roughly 2,500 kilometers, with relay stations so that royal couriers and armies could move quickly. Standardized coinage and regular tribute tied the provinces to the center, and the ruler styled himself "King of Kings," the head of many peoples rather than the destroyer of them.

AP Thinking, Causation

Persia's tolerance was a governing strategy, not just generosity. By letting conquered peoples keep their own ways and rulers, Persia reduced the reasons to rebel, solving the diversity and legitimacy problems at once and making a huge multiethnic empire governable.

3

Han China

Han China: Bureaucracy and the Mandate of Heaven

Where Persia delegated, Han China (206 BCE–220 CE) centralized. It governed some 60 million people through a trained bureaucracy of officials, increasingly chosen for their education in Confucian texts rather than for noble birth. Legitimacy rested on the Mandate of Heaven: Heaven granted authority to rulers who governed justly, and withdrew it, through disasters and successful rebellion, from those who did not.

This system made the Han immensely powerful: it pushed back the Xiongnu nomads on the northern frontier and opened the Silk Roads. But centralization had limits. When the Han over-taxed, let flood-control canals decay, and let court corruption spread, some 400,000 peasants launched the Yellow Turban Rebellion in 184 CE, invoking the very Mandate of Heaven to justify revolt. The dynasty crushed the rising only by empowering regional warlords, who then refused to disband and tore China apart.

AP Thinking, Continuity & Change

The Mandate of Heaven was a continuity across thousands of years of Chinese history, but it cut both ways. It legitimized a just ruler and it justified overthrowing an unjust one. Bureaucracy made the Han strong; the same dependence on the center made it brittle when the center failed.

4

Greece & Rome

The Mediterranean: Citizens, Law, and Empire

Classical Greece was not a single state but hundreds of small, independent city-states, or poleis. The Greeks pioneered a radically different answer to the power problem: rule by citizens. Athens developed direct democracy, in which free adult male citizens debated and voted on laws in person; Sparta built a militarized society run by a small ruling elite. But Greek "citizen rule" was narrow, women, enslaved people, and foreign residents (metics), who together were the majority, had no vote. Power was shared, but only among a minority.

Rome scaled these ideas up. The Roman Republic mixed elected consuls, a powerful Senate, and popular assemblies to spread authority and check any single man. As Rome conquered the Mediterranean, that system strained, and after decades of civil war Augustus turned the Republic into the Roman Empire, ruled by an emperor. Rome's genius was integration: a shared body of Roman law and the gradual extension of Roman citizenship to conquered peoples gave distant provinces a stake in the system, while roads and professional legions held it together. Its limits showed too, the enslaved gladiator Spartacus led over 70,000 people in revolt (73–71 BCE), and Rome crushed it brutally.

AP Thinking, Argumentation

Greece and Rome offer two more answers: sharing authority among citizens, and binding conquered peoples through law and citizenship. A strong argument names the tool, democracy, Roman law, citizenship, and explains both what it accomplished and whom it left out.

5

Significance

Four Answers, One Set of Questions

Line the four up. Persia ruled a diverse empire by tolerating difference and delegating to satraps. Han China ruled by centralized bureaucracy backed by the Mandate of Heaven. The Greek poleis experimented with citizen self-rule on a small scale. Rome combined republican checks, then imperial authority, with law and citizenship to integrate an empire. Four civilizations, four solutions to the same problems of extraction, defense, administration, and legitimacy.

And every one of them hit limits and met resistance, the Yellow Turbans against the Han, Spartacus against Rome, and the vast populations that citizenship and citizenship-style democracy simply excluded. These classical models became the toolkit later states drew on, and the four questions are exactly the ones you will ask of every state in AP World, from Song China to the Abbasid Caliphate to the Aztec and Inca empires.

AP Thinking, Comparison

Comparison is the move this course rewards. Don't just describe one state, set two side by side, name the different tools they used, and explain why each fit the problem that state faced. Persia's satraps and Han China's bureaucracy were two answers to the very same question.

BeReady: 10-Second Takeaway

Between 550 BCE and 220 CE, four classical civilizations answered one question, how do a few hold power over many? Persia delegated to satraps and tolerated local ways; Han China built a centralized bureaucracy legitimized by the Mandate of Heaven; the Greek poleis shared power among citizens (though most people were excluded); Rome integrated an empire through law and citizenship, shifting from republic to emperor. Every one relied on tools of rule, every one built a social hierarchy, and every one faced resistance. Those are the patterns AP World asks you to see in states everywhere.
