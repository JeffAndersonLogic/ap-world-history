First & 10 Reading

Three Networks That Moved the World

Before c.1200, three great trade networks stitched Afro-Eurasia together: the overland Silk Roads, the monsoon-driven Indian Ocean, and the trans-Saharan caravan routes. They moved silk, spices, and gold. But they also moved religions, technologies, crops, and diseases. Trade routes were never only about goods; they were the circulatory system of the premodern world.

Contextualization

Trade Routes Move More Than Goods

A trade network is a system of routes and relationships that carries goods between distant societies. But the goods are only the most visible layer. Along with silk and salt travel merchants and missionaries, seeds and livestock, tools and techniques, and, invisibly, the germs those travelers carry. What moves through a trade network is never just the cargo.

Before c.1200, three great networks did most of this work across Afro-Eurasia: the overland Silk Roads, the maritime Indian Ocean system, and the trans-Saharan caravan routes. They were not three separate worlds. Together they formed a single connected system, and learning how each one worked, and what rode along with the goods, is the key to reading every region of AP World.

AP Thinking, Causation

The historian's move is to look past the cargo. Ask what else rode along with the goods, because the invisible cargo (a religion, a technology, a disease) often changed history more than the silk did.

2

The Silk Roads

A Relay Across Asia

The Silk Roads were not a single road. They were a branching relay network of overland routes running from the Chinese capital of Chang'an, through the oasis cities of Central Asia, on to Samarkand, Persia, and the Mediterranean. No single merchant traveled the whole way. Goods changed hands again and again at oasis towns, passing from Chinese to Sogdian to Persian to Arab middlemen, each taking a profit and handing off the risk of the next leg.

This relay carried silk, porcelain, and (after 751 CE) paper westward, and horses, glassware, spices, and precious metals eastward. It also carried people and ideas: Buddhist monks traveled with the caravans, and merchants funded monasteries at oasis stops as spiritual insurance for their dangerous journeys.

AP Thinking, Continuity & Change

The Silk Roads operated for more than a thousand years. What changed was the political conditions around them: when strong states like Tang China (and later the Mongols) kept the routes safe, trade surged; when they fragmented, it shrank. The network endured; its volume rose and fell with the powers along it.

3

The Indian Ocean

Sailing on the Monsoon

The Indian Ocean network ran on the monsoon winds, which reverse with the seasons. The southwest monsoon (June to September) pushed ships from Arabia and East Africa toward India and Southeast Asia; the northeast monsoon (November to March) carried them home. This reliable reversal created a natural round-trip schedule, and it made the Indian Ocean the world's first sustained maritime trade network.

Wealthy port cities grew at the junctions of these sea-lanes: Aden, Hormuz, Calicut, Malacca, Quanzhou, and Kilwa. The Srivijaya Empire on Sumatra grew rich by controlling the Strait of Malacca, the chokepoint every ship between India and China had to pass. On the far side, Chinese porcelain excavated at Kilwa in East Africa proves that this single network reached across four continents.

AP Thinking, Causation

Geography set the schedule. Because the winds reversed on a dependable calendar, merchants could plan annual round trips without fighting the wind, which is exactly why the Indian Ocean became the richest maritime network of the premodern world.

4

Trans-Saharan Routes

Turning a Desert into a Highway

For most of history the Sahara was a barrier. What changed it was a technology: the camel saddle, adopted around 300 CE, which let camels carry heavy loads for days without water. The desert became a highway of caravan routes linking West African goldfields to North African and Mediterranean markets. The trade ran on complementary needs, gold moving north and Saharan salt moving south.

The empires of Ghana and then Mali grew wealthy by taxing every load that crossed their territory. After Islam spread across North and West Africa, it supplied the commercial glue: a shared body of contract law, a credit system that moved value without moving gold, and caravanserais that sheltered merchants across thousands of kilometers of desert.

AP Thinking, Continuity & Change

The Sahara itself did not change, but the tools people brought to it did. A technology (the camel saddle) and an institution (Islamic commercial law) turned a barrier into a highway. Sometimes what transforms a trade route is not the route but what people bring to it.

5

Significance

What Else Moved, and Why It Matters

The most consequential cargo was often invisible. Technology: Chinese papermakers captured at the Battle of Talas (751 CE) carried papermaking into the Islamic world, where cheap paper fueled an extraordinary flowering of scholarship. Religion: Buddhism spread into China with Silk Road merchants, and Islam reached Southeast Asia through Indian Ocean traders rather than armies. Crops: fast-maturing Champa rice traveled from Vietnam to Song China, enabling two harvests a year and helping the population soar. Disease: the Plague of Justinian (541 CE) rode grain ships to Constantinople, killing tens of millions.

This is why historians treat the three networks as one circulatory system rather than three separate roads. Goods, people, ideas, crops, and germs all moved together. By c.1200 that system's pulse was strong enough to sustain the most commercially sophisticated economies on earth, and it set the stage for everything AP World studies next.

AP Thinking, Argumentation

A strong argument about trade names the network, names what moved (a good AND a non-good), and explains the consequence. "The Indian Ocean carried both pepper and Islam to Southeast Asia" is an argument; "there was a lot of trade" is not.

BeReady: 10-Second Takeaway

Three networks connected Afro-Eurasia before c.1200: the overland Silk Roads (a relay of oasis cities), the Indian Ocean (sailing the reversing monsoon winds), and the trans-Saharan caravans (unlocked by the camel saddle and Islamic commercial law). Each moved goods, but the invisible cargo mattered most: paper and other technologies, Buddhism and Islam, Champa rice, and deadly plague all traveled the same routes. Trade networks are systems that transform whole societies, not pipelines that only move goods.
