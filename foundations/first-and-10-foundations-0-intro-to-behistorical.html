Frame

You Are Not Here to Memorize

Every year on Day 1, you walk into a room and become someone. This year, in this room, you become a historian. Not in the sense that you memorize kings and dates — that is not what historians do. A historian is someone who reads the past the way a detective reads a crime scene: closely, skeptically, evidence in hand, ready to make an argument and defend it.

You will still learn a lot of history in this class. But the history is the material you practice on — not the point of the class. The point is who you become while practicing on it. AP World History does not test your ability to recall dates in May. It tests your ability to think like a historian. That is a very specific set of habits, and this course exists to build them into you.

AP Thinking — Argumentation

The AP exam does not reward memorization. It rewards the ability to make an argument about the past — a claim in your voice, supported by evidence, that another intelligent person could reasonably disagree with. Every module in this course is a rep for that skill.

2

The Six Habits

The Six AP Historical Thinking Skills

AP World History is built around six specific habits of mind — the six things historians actually do when they think. Learn them by name, because you will see them in every module of every lesson this year.

Contextualization is placing something inside the world that surrounded it, before you try to explain it. Set the stage first. Comparison is reading two things side by side to see what is the same, what is different, and — the AP kicker — why the difference matters. Never just list similarities.

Causation is following the chain of cause and effect, and being able to show how you know. Historians distinguish between triggers (the spark) and underlying causes (the fuel). Both matter. Neither alone explains. Continuity and change over time is watching what stayed the same and what changed as history moved forward — and refusing the easy story that everything changed or nothing did. Both are almost always wrong.

Argumentation is making a claim about the past in your own voice and backing it with specific evidence. A thesis is a position, not a summary. If someone cannot disagree with your thesis, it is not one. And sourcing is reading a document the way a lawyer reads a witness — who made it, when, why, for whom — and letting that shape how you weigh what it says.

AP Thinking — Contextualization

These six skills are the whole game. Every unit, every lesson, every module in BeHistorical is a rep for one of these six habits. You will do them so many times that by May they become the way you think, not just tasks you finish. That is what “becoming a historian” means. It is not a metaphor.

The Rhythm

The Ten Modules of Every Class Period

The rhythm of every class period is the same 10-module pattern, and it is deliberate. Map builds geographic context. First & 10 is the day’s narrative reading — the historian’s warm-up, which is what you are doing right now. Content Delivery is direct instruction. BeSurreal is a memorable everyday-life detail from the historical moment — the sensory anchor that makes the concept stick.

Then the practice work. Skill Builder practices one AP skill on today’s content. Checkpoint 1 captures your thinking early. Evidence Lab treats visuals as evidence, not decoration. Primary Source (or the Socrates AI Coach in Foundations) trains the sourcing skill. BeInTheRoom puts you inside the moment as a decision-maker — a general, an emperor, a merchant, a scholar — and asks what you would do and why. And Checkpoint 2 is a synthesis, pulling threads across the modules into a single argument.

The modules repeat because the skills repeat. You are building muscle memory. The room does not change every class. The work you do inside it does.

AP Thinking — Continuity & Change Over Time

The 10-module pattern is the continuity across the year. What changes is your ability to move through it. By January, the checkpoints get faster and sharper. By April, the BeInTheRoom decisions sound like they were made by someone who lives inside the moment. That change is the record of who you became.

4

The Promise

What You Are Building: Your Historian’s Portfolio

Here is what makes BeHistorical different from most classes: everything you write this year accumulates. Every First & 10 response. Every checkpoint. Every BeInTheRoom decision. Every DBQ draft. Your writing is the raw material for what comes next.

In May, after the AP exam, you receive your Historian’s Portfolio: a one-page letter about the historian you became, drawn from your own writing at three moments across the year — September, January, April. The letter will name what you got specifically better at. It will flag one growth edge to keep working on. And it will quote actual sentences you wrote at different points in the year, so the story is grounded in evidence you can see with your own eyes.

The portfolio is yours. You see the draft before it is final. You can edit it. You choose who sees it — you, your family, your next AP teacher if you want, your college applications in senior year. It contains only your thinking. Never behavior notes, never personality labels, never anything you did not write. If it is not in your words on the page, it is not in the letter.

This is the receipt for what happened to your mind this year. It is not a grade. It is a portable, permanent artifact of who you became — and it starts today, with whatever you write in Checkpoint 2 of this lesson.

AP Thinking — Sourcing

You are being asked, on Day 1, to become the source of the most important document about this year of your life. Write like someone will read it in May. Because someone will. You.

BeReady: 10-Second Takeaway

You are not here to memorize. You are here to become a historian — someone who does six specific habits of mind, in a room that runs on a 10-module rhythm — and to leave a year of thinking behind that you own, in a portfolio drawn from your own words. The record starts today.

For Families

A Note to Parents & Guardians

Welcome to BeHistorical. This year your student is not just enrolled in AP World History — they are being asked to become a historian. That means practicing six specific habits of mind (contextualization, comparison, causation, continuity and change over time, argumentation, and sourcing) across every unit of the course, and producing a body of writing along the way that captures how they think.

In May, after the AP exam, your student will receive a Historian’s Portfolio — a one-page letter about the historian they became this year, drawn from their own writing at three moments across the year. You will receive a family copy. Here is what you should know about how it works:

  • Nothing without evidence. Every claim in the portfolio quotes something your student actually wrote. If it is not in their words, it is not in the letter.
  • Academic voice only. The portfolio contains only your student’s historical thinking — never behavior notes, never discipline history, never personality labels.
  • Your student reviews the draft. They see it before it is final. They can edit or remove anything. Their voice is their own.
  • Consent-based sharing. The portfolio does not go anywhere without your student’s permission. Next-year teacher, college application, personal archive — your student decides.

Ask your student about their portfolio through the year. Ask what they wrote in September. Ask what they would write about the same topic now. That is how you will hear this course working.

Check Three Questions to Anchor Day 1
  • 01 Contextualization

    In your own words, define one of the six AP historical thinking skills and give a specific example — from anywhere in your life, not from history — of a time you already used it without calling it that.

  • 02 Argumentation

    Take a position: which of the six historical thinking skills do you predict will be hardest for you this year, and why? Be honest and be specific — the answer is a baseline, not a grade.

  • 03 Sourcing

    Your Historian’s Portfolio in May will be sourced from your own words this year — the way historians build claims from documents. Write one sentence you would like your portfolio to be able to say honestly about you, and identify one thing you will have to write this year for that sentence to be sourceable from the record.
