Contextualization

The Promise That Wasn't Kept: Self-Determination After WWI

When World War I ended in 1918, U.S. President Woodrow Wilson announced his Fourteen Points, a framework for a just peace. Point Fourteen created the League of Nations. But scattered through Wilson's plan was an idea that electrified people from Egypt to India to West Africa: self-determination. Peoples, Wilson suggested, should have the right to choose their own form of government. For colonized peoples who had supplied armies, labor, and resources to European powers throughout the war, this was more than an abstract principle. It felt like a promise.

The Paris Peace Conference of 1919 delivered something different. The Allied powers, Britain, France, Italy, Japan, and the United States, divided Germany's former colonies and the territories of the collapsed Ottoman Empire among themselves through the League of Nations Mandate System. Britain gained control over Palestine, Iraq, and Tanganyika. France took Syria, Lebanon, and parts of West Africa. The Mandate System gave European powers formal authority over millions of people in Asia, Africa, and the Middle East, using the language of "trusteeship" and "civilizing mission" to dress up what was, in practice, an expansion of colonial control.

Between the wars, nationalist movements organized across the colonized world. The Indian National Congress, led by figures including Mohandas Gandhi and Jawaharlal Nehru, built a mass independence movement rooted in civil disobedience and noncooperation with British rule. Pan-African activists organized congresses demanding recognition of African peoples' political rights. Vietnamese nationalists agitated against French rule. In each case, European colonial governments responded with a combination of token concessions and repression. The movements were weakened, co-opted, or suppressed, but not destroyed.

AP Thinking, Contextualization

Before you can explain the Cold War or decolonization, you need to understand why 1945 felt so charged to people living in colonized countries. The gap between Wilson's self-determination rhetoric and the Mandate System's reality left a reservoir of grievance that World War II would turn into action. Contextualization for Topic 8.1 means tracing that connection: the interwar period is part of the context that made the post-1945 world.

Key Concept, KC-6.2.II

Empires Come Home to Collect: WWII and the Cost of Empire

World War II did not just kill soldiers. It exhausted economies. Britain entered the war as the center of the world's largest empire. It emerged in 1945 with debts it could not repay, a treasury that was nearly empty, and a military stretched across dozens of territories it could no longer afford to police. The United States had terminated Lend-Lease aid to Britain in September 1945, weeks after the war ended, forcing Britain to negotiate a painful American loan whose terms required dismantling imperial trade preferences. The empire's fiscal foundation was gone.

In this context, independence movements that had been suppressed for decades suddenly faced a weakened adversary. India was the first and most dramatic case. The Indian National Congress, strengthened by decades of organizing, combined mass civil disobedience with political negotiations that the exhausted British government could not resist. In 1947, Britain announced it would transfer power, and Nehru became the first Prime Minister of an independent India. The partition of British India into India and Pakistan accompanied independence, producing one of the largest and most devastating mass migrations in human history.

Ghana followed a decade later, in 1957. Kwame Nkrumah, a pan-Africanist who had studied in the United States and Britain, organized a mass independence movement in the Gold Coast that Britain could not suppress without the resources it no longer had. Ghana's independence under Nkrumah was the first moment of decolonization in sub-Saharan Africa, and it sent a signal across the continent. By the early 1960s, dozens of African nations would follow.

The pattern was consistent: WWII had weakened European colonial powers, and independence movements that had been building for decades were now strong enough to succeed. The dissolution of empires and the restructuring of states across Asia and Africa, which the CED calls the result of increasing anti-imperialist sentiment, was the direct consequence of two intersecting forces: grievances that predated 1939, and a colonial system too exhausted after 1945 to hold on.

AP Thinking, Causation

Strong causation analysis identifies both immediate and underlying causes. The immediate cause of Indian independence in 1947 was British exhaustion after WWII. The underlying cause was decades of organizing by the Indian National Congress against a colonial system that had broken its own promise of self-determination. Both matter. AP questions on decolonization reward analysis that connects the short-term trigger (imperial exhaustion) to the long-term cause (anti-imperialist sentiment built since WWI).

Key Concept, KC-6.2.IV.C.i

Two Powers Standing: The Superpower Rivalry Emerges

While European empires were exhausting themselves, the United States and the Soviet Union were growing stronger. The United States entered WWII as a powerful industrial economy and emerged from it transformed. American GDP nearly doubled during the war years as factories shifted from consumer goods to weapons, aircraft, and ships. By 1945, the United States held roughly half of the world's industrial output, controlled significant nuclear technology, and had military forces positioned across Europe, the Pacific, and beyond. The war had done to the U.S. economy what it had done to Britain's and France's economies in reverse: it made it stronger.

The Soviet Union paid a catastrophic human cost, an estimated 27 million deaths, but it also emerged transformed. Wartime industrialization had dramatically expanded Soviet industrial capacity, particularly in the Ural mountains and Siberia, far from German advance. The Red Army that defeated Nazi Germany was the largest land force in the world, and Soviet scientists would develop nuclear weapons by 1949. The Soviet Union emerged as the world's other superpower, holding an ideology, Marxism-Leninism, that promised liberation to colonized and exploited peoples everywhere, making it an attractive alternative to the Western capitalist order for nationalist movements in Asia and Africa.

The two powers had fundamentally different visions for the post-war world, and those visions immediately collided. The United States launched the Marshall Plan in 1948, committing $13 billion in economic aid to rebuild Western Europe, and to anchor it within the American sphere of influence. The Soviet Union extended its control over Eastern Europe through communist governments installed in Poland, Czechoslovakia, Hungary, Romania, Bulgaria, and East Germany. These two blocs, one centered on Washington, one on Moscow, formalized into NATO (1949) and the Warsaw Pact (1955), military alliances that divided Europe and much of the world into rival camps.

The Cold War that resulted was not a single event. It was a structural condition, a state of continuous competition, ideological rivalry, proxy conflict, and arms buildup, that shaped global politics for the next four decades. Understanding it requires understanding where it came from: the technological and economic gains of the victorious nations after WWII had fundamentally shifted the global balance of power, creating two superpowers with rival ideologies and the capacity to act on them globally.

AP Thinking, Contextualization

The Cold War did not begin in 1947 or 1949. It had roots that reached back through WWII, the interwar period, and beyond. Explaining the historical context of the Cold War after 1945 means connecting at least two broader developments: the shift in the balance of power that WWII produced, and the unresolved tensions from the failure of post-WWI self-determination. Both sets of conditions mattered. The Cold War emerged in a world shaped by both.

BeReady: 10-Second Takeaway

Three forces set the stage for the post-1945 world: (1) unfulfilled promises of self-determination after WWI created lasting anti-imperialist sentiment; (2) WWII exhausted Britain and France, weakening their ability to maintain colonial empires and accelerating decolonization; (3) WWII's technological and economic gains produced two rival superpowers, the U.S. and the Soviet Union, whose competition for global influence defined the Cold War. Decolonization and the Cold War were not separate stories. They happened simultaneously, shaped each other, and both grew from the same post-WWII moment.
